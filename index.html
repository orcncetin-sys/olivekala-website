Origin & Science · October 2025

The case for early harvest — why we pick before peak ripeness

Olivekala Journal  ·  5 min read  ·  Ayvalık, Turkey

Most olive oil producers wait. They let the Memecik fruit ripen fully because a mature olive gives more volume per tree — sometimes 20–30% more oil by weight than a green, early-picked fruit. The economic logic is obvious. The quality logic runs the other way.

At 10–20% ripeness, before the olive has completed its sugar and fatty-acid cycle, two things are true simultaneously: free acidity is at its lowest, and polyphenol concentration is at its highest. These two values move in opposite directions as the fruit matures — acidity rises as enzymatic activity accelerates, polyphenols fall as the olive converts them into pigment and energy for ripening.

The chemistry of the green fruit

Free acidity in olive oil is a measure of degradation — specifically the proportion of free fatty acids produced when the fruit's cell walls break down. A freshly picked, undamaged green olive has minimal enzymatic activity and almost no cell-wall breakdown. Its free fatty acid content is correspondingly low. By the time the same fruit reaches full purple-black ripeness, enzymatic activity has been running for weeks. Every bruise, every delay, every degree of temperature above ambient accelerates the process.

Polyphenols — specifically oleocanthal, oleuropein, and hydroxytyrosol — are the compounds responsible for the peppery, bitter finish that distinguishes premium EVOO from neutral commercial oil. They are also potent antioxidants, which means they protect the oil's structure during cooking and storage. Early-picked Memecik registers polyphenol concentrations above 300 mg/kg. The same grove's oil at full ripeness: typically 80–120 mg/kg.

The yield trade-off

We lose volume. An early-picked tree gives roughly 12–15% oil by weight. A fully ripe fruit gives 18–22%. Over a grove of several thousand trees, the difference is significant. We accept it because the alternative is an oil that meets the letter of the IOC's extra virgin standard while failing its spirit. The IOC ceiling for free acidity is 0.8%. Most commercial oils sit at 0.5–0.7% — technically extra virgin, perceptibly ordinary.

"The early pick is a decision to make less oil and better oil. In a market where most decisions run the other way, that requires a specific kind of conviction."

KALA's October harvest is the only harvest we run. There is no second picking, no late-season volume batch, no blending with mid-season fruit to reduce the financial cost of the early pick. One press window. One specification. The tin in your kitchen is the direct result of that decision.

Quality & Specification · November 2025

What 0.2% free acidity actually means on the pass

Olivekala Journal  ·  6 min read  ·  Ayvalık, Turkey

The number 0.2% appears on the KALA tin, on every batch certificate, and in every conversation we have with buyers. It requires explanation — not because it is complicated, but because most people have no reference point for what it means in practice.

The International Olive Council sets 0.8% as the maximum free acidity permitted for extra virgin classification. It is a generous ceiling. A surveyor of shelves would find that most oils marketed as premium extra virgin sit between 0.5% and 0.7% — compliant, but nowhere near the top of the achievable range. At 0.2%, we are sitting at roughly a quarter of that ceiling.

What free acidity is, precisely

Free acidity is not a flavour compound directly — it is a marker of degradation. Oleic acid, the primary fatty acid in olive oil, remains bound in triglyceride form in an intact, freshly pressed olive. The moment degradation begins — through enzymatic activity in bruised fruit, oxidation during storage, or excessive heat during extraction — fatty acids cleave from their glycerol backbone and become "free." The percentage of these unbound fatty acids is the free acidity figure.

An oil at 0.7% has undergone significantly more degradation than an oil at 0.2% — it has been in the degradation process longer, at higher temperatures, or from more damaged fruit. The palate detects this indirectly: as a flat, slightly greasy quality in the mid-palate, a short finish, an absence of the structural bitterness that marks genuinely fresh oil.

On the pass: what actually changes

A chef working with an oil at 0.2% is working with a structurally intact product. The polyphenols that drive flavour complexity are present and active. The oil tolerates moderate heat better than a higher-acidity alternative — the antioxidant load that accompanies low acidity provides genuine structural protection at temperature. The finish is longer, with a measurable pepper note at the back of the throat that trained palates associate with freshness and polyphenol density.

"The difference between 0.4% and 0.2% is not a number on a certificate. It is a quality decision made at every point between the grove and the tin."

Every KALA batch is independently tested and certified. The certificate accompanies every commercial order. The number does not shift between seasons because the decisions that produce it — early pick, same-day press, cold extraction — do not shift either. The specification is not an aspiration. It is the outcome of a controlled process, repeated to the same standard each October.

Geography · December 2025

Ayvalık: why 38.7° north latitude is the point

Olivekala Journal  ·  5 min read  ·  Ayvalık, Turkey

Ayvalık is not the intuitive choice. The olive's heartland runs from Andalusia through Tunisia to the Levant — warm, predictable, high-yield growing conditions that produce consistent, voluminous harvests. Ayvalık sits at the northern edge of the olive's viable range. The winters are cooler, the growing season is shorter, and the Memecik variety grown here matures slowly under conditions that would simply be suboptimal for any producer optimising for yield.

That suboptimality is the point.

Stress physiology and flavour

Plants under moderate environmental stress produce secondary metabolites as a protective response. In olives, that response manifests primarily as polyphenol synthesis — the same compounds that give high-quality EVOO its bitterness, its pepper finish, and its antioxidant load. A Memecik olive grown in Ayvalık's cooler northern conditions builds polyphenols at a higher rate than the same variety grown in a warmer, more forgiving climate.

The mechanism is analogous to what happens with alpine herbs versus lowland equivalents, or with high-altitude coffee versus valley-grown beans. Slower maturation, cooler nights, and the marginal stress of a difficult growing environment concentrate flavour compounds in ways that optimal conditions do not replicate.

The Memecik cultivar specifically

Memecik is a Turkish heritage variety rarely grown outside the Aegean region. It is not widely known internationally — it has not been aggressively marketed the way Arbequina or Picual have, and it lacks the commercial infrastructure of the Italian or Greek designations. What it has is a flavour profile that chefs who encounter it tend to find arresting: a pronounced green, slightly grassy character in early harvest, a firm bitterness, and a finish that outlasts most competing varieties.

"The grove has been here for three thousand years. We are simply the latest people to understand what it produces when you harvest it correctly."

The combination of Ayvalık's latitude, the Memecik cultivar's natural polyphenol tendency, and an early-harvest protocol produces an oil that could not be replicated by moving the production south or switching the variety. The geography is not incidental to the specification. It is the specification.

Kitchen Application · January 2026

Cooking with high-polyphenol oil — what the heat does

Olivekala Journal  ·  7 min read  ·  Ayvalık, Turkey

The standard advice is to keep premium extra virgin olive oil away from heat. Use it for finishing, for dressings, for the table — but not for cooking. This advice was written for standard commercial oil and has been applied wholesale to all extra virgin, regardless of specification. It is, for high-polyphenol oil, partially wrong.

The smoke point of extra virgin olive oil is typically cited at 190–210°C. This is accurate for most commercial EVOO, where the free acidity is 0.5–0.8% and the polyphenol count is low. At 0.2% free acidity and 300+ mg/kg polyphenols, the structural chemistry is different.

Why polyphenols matter at temperature

Polyphenols in EVOO function as natural antioxidants. Their role in the bottle is to slow oxidative degradation during storage. Their role in the pan is analogous: they scavenge free radicals produced during heating, protecting the oil's fatty-acid structure from oxidation. An oil with a high polyphenol load — particularly oleocanthal and hydroxytyrosol — degrades more slowly under heat than a structurally equivalent oil with lower polyphenol content.

This does not mean KALA oil should be used for deep frying at 180°C as a primary cooking medium. It means that moderate-heat applications — sautéing aromatics, building a sauce base, finishing a protein in the pan — do not destroy the oil's character the way they would with a fragile, lower-specification product.

Practical kitchen applications

Where we have seen chefs use KALA most effectively under heat: building soffritto bases where olive character should persist into the finished dish; pan-finishing fish, where a 30-second high-heat contact needs an oil that does not burn before the protein is done; in pasta cooking water — a small addition that seasons without the soapy quality of lower-grade oil at temperature.

"The finishing argument has been overstated. A structurally sound oil handles a hot pan differently to a fragile one. Test it before you assume."

We do not claim that KALA performs as a neutral cooking oil. We claim that its specification makes it more versatile than the standard advice suggests — and that a kitchen using it only as a finishing oil is leaving half its potential on the shelf.

Supply Chain · February 2026

Why four hours matters — the oxidation window

Olivekala Journal  ·  6 min read  ·  Ayvalık, Turkey

The moment an olive breaks from its branch, degradation begins. This is not metaphor or marketing language — it is the biochemistry of a fruit that has evolved to begin fermenting and decomposing as soon as it separates from its parent tree. Cell walls rupture under the pressure of handling. Polyphenol oxidase, an enzyme present in the flesh, begins converting polyphenols into melanin — the same browning process visible when you cut an apple. Lipase enzymes begin cleaving fatty acids from their triglyceride bonds, incrementally raising free acidity.

All of this happens whether or not you can see it. The olives in the harvest bin look fine. The process is running.

What happens each hour

Quantifying the degradation rate is not straightforward — temperature, harvest handling method, and the ambient humidity all affect the speed. As a general approximation based on published IOC research and our own batch testing over the 2025 harvest: free acidity increases by roughly 0.03–0.06 percentage points per hour under ambient Aegean October conditions (approximately 18–22°C). Polyphenol concentration falls faster — an estimated 8–12% reduction per four-hour window beyond the first press opportunity.

Overnight storage — the industry norm

The standard industry practice is overnight fruit storage — harvested fruit is collected during the day and pressed the following morning. This is logistically sensible and economically efficient. It also means the fruit has been degrading for eight to sixteen hours before it reaches the press. For a producer optimising for IOC compliance rather than specification, this is entirely acceptable. Free acidity will still be within the 0.8% ceiling. The polyphenol count will be lower, the structural freshness will be diminished, but the certificate will be valid.

"Every hour between the grove and the press is a decision — to prioritise logistics over specification. We made that decision once, in one direction, and we have not revisited it."

The four-hour rule requires running the press in shifts during harvest, absorbing the higher operational cost of continuous extraction, and accepting the logistical constraints of a same-day commitment. The alternative is a better-organised harvest that produces inferior oil. We chose the harder schedule.

Can a catering oil be a quality oil? The argument for not accepting the trade-off

Olivekala Journal  ·  6 min read  ·  Ayvalık, Turkey

The assumption is structural: catering format implies bulk pricing implies reduced specification. This is the logic that has produced an entire category of catering oils — technically extra virgin, commercially functional, professionally indistinguishable from neutral vegetable oil at temperature. The category exists because someone, somewhere in each supply chain, accepted the trade-off.

We do not accept it as structurally necessary. Our argument is that the trade-off is a choice, not an inevitability — and that the choice has almost always been made on the wrong side of the quality line because the buyers in institutional procurement are not the people eating the food.

The specification argument

KALA's 15L catering drum is filled from the same October press as the numbered 500ml Harvest Edition. The same Memecik fruit, the same cold-extraction run, the same batch tested against the same IOC parameters. Free acidity: 0.2%. Polyphenols: above 300 mg/kg. Peroxide value: below 12 meq O₂/kg. The certificate that accompanies the 15L drum and the certificate that accompanies the 500ml bottle are drawn from the same batch data.

The format changes. The oil does not.

Why this matters for large operations

A hotel group running five properties through a central procurement function is making an oil decision that affects every plate of food served across those properties for the year. If that decision defaults to the catering category's standard specification — free acidity at 0.6%, polyphenols at 80 mg/kg — the gap between what the kitchen could be producing and what it is producing is significant, measurable, and invisible to the procurement function that made the decision.

"The chef at the pass is not involved in the procurement decision. The person who made that decision is not at the pass. This information gap is where quality goes to die."

We supply catering operations directly, with full batch documentation, on the basis that the decision-maker should see the same numbers the chef would care about if they were consulted. In our experience, when a procurement director sees the actual specification comparison rather than just a price-per-litre table, the conversation changes. The oil in the 15L drum is the argument. The price is the footnote.
